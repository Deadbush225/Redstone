Capillary action is a fascinating phenomenon that occurs when a liquid spontaneously flows into a narrow tube or porous material, without requiring the force of gravity. Let’s dive into the details:

Factors Affecting the Capillary Action:

Capillary action, also known as capillarity, capillary motion, or wicking, arises due to the interplay of different forces:

1. Surface Tension

Capillary action is influenced by surface tension, which is the result of cohesive forces between like molecules forming a surface that resists penetration

The vapour pressure decreases with an increase in the magnitude of intermolecular forces. When the magnitude of intermolecular forces decreases, the vapor pressure of the liquid increases. For example, at a particular temperature, the vapor pressure of glycerol is less than that of isopentane. This is because the magnitude of the intermolecular forces is higher in glycerol.

2. Liquid’s Boiling Point

The boiling point is also a crucial factor that affects the vapor pressure. The relationship between boiling point and vapor pressure is inversely proportional. It means that the vapor pressure of the liquid with a higher boiling point will have a lower vapor pressure.

For example, when the vapor pressures of ethyl alcohol and ether are compared, the vapour pressure of ether is higher because it has a lower boiling point.

3. Temperature

Temperature is the most significant factor that affects vapor pressure. When a liquid is provided with a high temperature, the kinetic energy of its molecules increases.

Molecules possessing higher kinetic energy tend to leave the liquid surface more quickly, and the vapor pressure will increase due to more molecules getting converted into vapor.

Hence, we can conclude that temperature is directly proportional to the vapor pressure of a liquid. To be precise, the vapor pressure increases with an increase in temperature and vice-versa.

When these forces combine, they pull the liquid into the tube or material, allowing it to rise against gravity.

Examples:

  • Paper and Plaster: Capillary action causes water to be drawn into paper and plaster, both of which are porous materials.
  • Paintbrush Wicking: Ever notice how paint travels between the bristles of a paintbrush? That’s capillary action at work.
  • Water Movement Through Sand: Capillarity enables water to move through sand particles.
  • Celery Stalk Experiment: Try placing a celery stalk in colored water. Observe how the dye travels up the stalk due to capillary action.
  • White Carnations: Similarly, you can color white carnations by placing them in dyed water. The color migrates to the petals via capillary action.
  • Paper Towel Wicking: When you use a paper towel to mop up a spill, it’s capillary action that draws the liquid upward.
