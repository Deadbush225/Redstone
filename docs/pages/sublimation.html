Sublimation is a physical process that allows a solid to change from a solid state into a gas or vapor without going through a liquid phase.

This process happens when a material is heated to a point where the solid's vapor pressure rises to a level that allows molecules to separate from the solid's surface and enter the gas phase.

Factors affecting Molar Heat of Vaporization:

Sublimation is a kind of phase transition in which a gas instantly turns into a solid, but the speed can change due to the influence of other factors such as:

1. Temperature

Heat is usually used to start the sublimation process, and the strength of a substance's intermolecular forces determines the temperature at which it will sublimate.

In gases, viscosity is directly proportional to temperature.

2. Pressure

The process of sublimation can also be impacted by ambient pressure. Sublimation can only occur at higher pressures if the solid's vapor pressure is higher.

3. Heating Rate

The sublimation process can also be influenced by the rate at which heat is supplied to the solid. More consistent sublimation can result from a slower heating rate.

Rapid heating, on the other hand, may cause uneven sublimation causing the solid to develop cracks or other flaws.

WHO???

Joseph Priestley

There are several famous chemists known for studying sublimation, but one of the most well-known is Joseph Priestley. Priestley was an 18th-century British chemist who is best known for his discovery of oxygen and his studies of various gases, including carbon dioxide, ammonia, and sulfur dioxide.

He is also known for his work on the process of sublimation, which is the transition of a substance directly from a solid to a gas without passing through the liquid phase.

Other Experiments:

1. Dry Ice

Solid carbon dioxide (dry ice) sublimes everywhere along the line below the triple point at pressures and temperatures above the triple point.

Whereas its melting into liquid CO2 can occur anywhere along the solid-liquid line at pressures and temperatures below the triple point.

2. Naphthalene

Sodium naphthalene, a non-polar chemical compound that is frequently present in pesticides like mothballs, quickly sublimes because it is made up of non-polar molecules and is only held together by the weak van der Waals intermolecular forces.

If the naphthalene vapors come into touch with chilly surfaces, they will condense into needle-like crystals.

3. Iodine

Iodine emits fumes when heated gently. Iodine vapor can reveal hidden fingerprints on paper in forensic science.
