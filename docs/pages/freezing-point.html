Freezing point is the specific temperature at which the liquid turns into a solid.

Factors affecting Molar Heat of Vaporization:

During freezing, the average energy of the molecules decreases as heat is removed, leading to the formation of a solid structure from the liquid state but can also be affected by the influence of other factors such as:

1. Types of Molecules

The types of molecules that compose a liquid influence its freezing point.

Greater freezing points are produced by stronger intermolecular forces, whereas lower freezing points are produced by weaker forces.

2. Pressure

A substance's freezing point can change in response to pressure changes. A lower freezing point is typically the result of a decrease in pressure, whereas a higher freezing point is the outcome of an increase in pressure.

3. Impurities

A substance's freezing point can be lowered by adding impurities.

Adding highly soluble impurities to water, for instance, leads to an increase or decrease in its melting point. The presence of impurities in a liquid has a notable effect on melting point.

Other Experiments:

1. Ice Cube Formation

When water is placed in the freezer, its temperature decreases until it hits the freezing point, which is when ice cubes formed. This process of freezing involves the conversion of liquid water into solid ice.

2. Candle Wax Solidification

The melted wax in a candle cools down and approaches its freezing point after the flame burns out. Wax completes its cycle as it transitions from liquid to solid by hardening once more as the temperature drops.

3. Chocolate Bar Cooling Experiment

Melt chocolate bars and pour them into molds for a cool experiment. The freezing point can be determined by keeping an eye on how long it takes the chocolate to harden entirely in the molds after placing them in the freezer.
