A liquid partially vaporizes into the space above it at any temperature until the vapor's pressure exerted reaches a characteristic value known as the liquid's vapor pressure at that temperature. Vapor bubbles form in the liquid and rise to the surface as the temperature rises, indicating a rise in vapor pressure.

Other Experiments:

1. Pressure Cooker

In practically every kitchen, pressure cooking is the most popular cooking technique. It is also the most typical instance of boiling in daily life. The water is heated inside a pressure cooker until it boils and produces steam.

2. Sugar Refining

To get crystalline sugar, the extracted sugarcane juice needs to be refined. There are times when cane juice or syrup must boil, and the boiling point is determined by the amount of sugar present.

3. Antifreeze

In essence, antifreeze is an additive that lowers the freezing point of a water-based liquid when it is added. A coolant works because it causes the system to boil over when the temperature of the coolant rises to its boiling point. It raises the liquids' boiling points and stops boilovers.

4. Boiling Milk

Since fat is lighter than water, it rises to the surface of heated milk along with protein in the form of cream. If milk is heated too much, however, the expansion of water vapor creates pressure that pushes up the creamy layer until the milk spills out. Despite the fact that the boiling points of water and milk are fairly close, milk has a slightly higher boiling point due to boiling point elevation.
