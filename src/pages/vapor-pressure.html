Vapor pressure, also known as vapor equilibrium pressure, can be defined as the pressure exerted (in a system featuring thermodynamic equilibrium) by a vapor with its condensed phases (solid or liquid) in a closed system at a given temperature. The equilibrium vapor pressure is known to serve as an indicator of the evaporation rate of a liquid. The propensity of particles to escape from the liquid (or a solid) is known to be related. A material that, at normal temperatures, has a high vapour pressure is generally referred to as a volatile material.

Factors Affecting the Vapor Pressure:

The pressure exhibited above a liquid surface by the vapour, also known as called vapor pressure is influenced by factors such as:

1. Nature of Liquid

The nature of liquid is one of the most critical factors that affect vapor pressure. The effect of the nature of the liquid can be explained based on the intermolecular forces.

The vapour pressure decreases with an increase in the magnitude of intermolecular forces. When the magnitude of intermolecular forces decreases, the vapor pressure of the liquid increases. For example, at a particular temperature, the vapor pressure of glycerol is less than that of isopentane. This is because the magnitude of the intermolecular forces is higher in glycerol.

2. Liquidâ€™s Boiling Point

The boiling point is also a crucial factor that affects the vapor pressure. The relationship between boiling point and vapor pressure is inversely proportional. It means that the vapor pressure of the liquid with a higher boiling point will have a lower vapor pressure.

For example, when the vapor pressures of ethyl alcohol and ether are compared, the vapour pressure of ether is higher because it has a lower boiling point.

3. Temperature

Temperature is the most significant factor that affects vapor pressure. When a liquid is provided with a high temperature, the kinetic energy of its molecules increases.

Molecules possessing higher kinetic energy tend to leave the liquid surface more quickly, and the vapor pressure will increase due to more molecules getting converted into vapor.

Hence, we can conclude that temperature is directly proportional to the vapor pressure of a liquid. To be precise, the vapor pressure increases with an increase in temperature and vice-versa.

At any given temperature for a particular solid or liquid, there is a pressure at which the vapor formed above the substance is in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid or solid form. This is the vapor pressure of the substance at that temperature. At equilibrium, the rate at which the solid or liquid evaporates is equal to the rate that the gas is condensing back to its original form. All solids and liquids have a vapor pressure, and this pressure is constant regardless of how much of the substance is present.

Other Experiments:

1. Tea Kettle

A tea kettle demonstrates vapor pressure in the best possible manner. When the water poured inside the kettle starts boiling, the number of water vapors inside the kettle begins to increase. The water vapors exert pressure on the lid of the kettle, thereby pushing it up.

2. Pressure Cooker

The principle of vapor pressure underpins the entire functioning of a pressure cooker. Water vapors develop when heat is applied to the ingredients put into the pressure cooker. The vapor pressure in the cookware tends to rise due to this.

3. Drying Clothes

The drying of clothes is an important phenomenon that makes use of the concept of vapor pressure. Mostly to dry clothes at a faster rate, they are subjected to an environment that has a high temperature. For instance, traditionally the clothes are dried by exposing them to the sunlight.
